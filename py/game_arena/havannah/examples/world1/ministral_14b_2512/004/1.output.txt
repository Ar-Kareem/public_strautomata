### Strategy for Havannah Policy (15x15 Hexagonal Grid)

The Havannah game on a 15x15 hexagonal grid is a complex variant of Hex with additional winning conditions (ring, bridge, fork). My policy will prioritize moves based on the following **multi-phase strategy**:

---

#### **1. Immediate Win Check (Phase 1)**
- **First**, check if the current move can complete a **ring**, **bridge**, or **fork** for the player (`me`). If so, play it immediately to win.
- **Ring**: A closed loop around any cell (including empty cells). The policy will look for 5-in-a-row or 6-in-a-row loops (since a ring requires at least 5 connected stones forming a loop).
- **Bridge**: A path connecting any two of the six corner cells (e.g., (0,0) to (0,14), (0,0) to (14,0), etc.). The policy will prioritize moves that complete such a connection.
- **Fork**: A path connecting any three edges of the board (excluding corners). The policy will look for moves that create a third edge connection while already having two.

---

#### **2. Block Opponent’s Immediate Win (Phase 2)**
- **Second**, check if the opponent (`opp`) can complete a **ring**, **bridge**, or **fork** in their next move. If so, block it by placing a stone in the critical cell.
- This is defensive and ensures the opponent cannot win on their turn.

---

#### **3. Hexagonal Connectivity (Phase 3)**
- **Third**, treat Havannah as a **Hex-like game** where the primary goal is to connect opposite edges (e.g., left to right or top to bottom).
- Use **Moser’s algorithm** (or a simplified version) to evaluate the best move for connecting edges. This involves:
  - **Edge connectivity**: Prioritize moves that improve the player’s edge connections (e.g., left to right, top to bottom).
  - **Center control**: If no immediate edge threats, play near the center to maintain balance.
  - **Avoiding opponent’s edge threats**: If the opponent is close to connecting an edge, disrupt their path.

---

#### **4. Ring and Bridge Potential (Phase 4)**
- **Fourth**, look for **potential rings** (5 or 6 stones in a loop) or **bridges** (paths connecting two corners) that the player can create.
- Use **pattern matching** (e.g., hexagonal loops, corner connections) to identify high-probability winning moves.
- Prefer moves that create **two potential threats** (e.g., two separate rings or a bridge + a ring).

---

#### **5. Fork Potential (Phase 5)**
- **Fifth**, look for **potential forks** (paths connecting three edges).
- Prioritize moves that create **two edge connections** while leaving room for a third.
- If the opponent is close to a fork, play defensively to prevent it.

---

#### **6. Fallback: Center and Symmetry (Phase 6)**
- If no immediate threats or opportunities, play **symmetrically** (mirror the opponent’s moves relative to the center) or toward the **center** to maintain balance.
- Avoid giving the opponent free edge connections.

---

#### **7. Randomness for Uncertainty (Phase 7)**
- If all else fails, introduce **controlled randomness** to avoid predictable patterns.
- Use a **weighted random selection** based on the above phases (e.g., 70% Hex-like, 20% ring/bridge, 10% random).

---

### Key Implementation Details:
1. **Hexagonal Neighbor Lookup**:
   - For any cell `(r, c)`, define its 6 neighbors (up, down, left, right, and the two diagonals) to traverse the grid.
   - Example: `(4, 1)` (b5) has neighbors `(3,1)`, `(5,1)`, `(3,0)`, `(4,0)`, `(4,2)`, `(3,2)`.

2. **Winning Structure Detection**:
   - **Ring**: Use BFS/DFS to check for closed loops around any cell. A ring is formed if 5 or 6 stones of the same player encircle a cell.
   - **Bridge**: Check if a move connects two corner cells (e.g., (0,0) to (0,14)) via a path of the player’s stones.
   - **Fork**: Check if a move creates a path that connects three edges (e.g., top, left, right).

3. **Opponent Blocking**:
   - If the opponent has 4 stones in a row (with a gap that can be closed), block the gap to prevent a ring.
   - If the opponent is about to connect two corners, block the critical cell in their path.

4. **Hexagonal Connectivity Evaluation**:
   - Use **distance-to-edge metrics** (e.g., how many moves away the player is from connecting opposite edges).
   - Prefer moves that reduce the opponent’s edge connectivity while increasing your own.

5. **Symmetry and Center Control**:
   - If the board is symmetric, mirror the opponent’s moves.
   - Otherwise, play toward the center to maintain balance.

6. **Performance Optimization**:
   - Precompute **valid moves** and **neighbor relationships** to avoid redundant calculations.
   - Use **bitmasking** or **numpy arrays** for efficient pathfinding.

    return neighbors
