### Strategy Overview

My policy will implement a **hybrid approach** combining **evaluation-based move ordering** with **basic tactical patterns** to efficiently select strong moves within the time constraints. Here's the breakdown:

1. **Checkmate Detection**: First, I'll check if any move leads to an immediate checkmate (using a simple recursive depth-1 search with quiescence).
2. **Material Advantage**: If no checkmate is found, prioritize moves that maximize material gain (captures) or prevent material loss.
3. **King Safety**: Evaluate king safety by checking if moves improve pawn structure around the king or reduce exposure.
4. **Development & Centralization**: Prefer moves that develop pieces, control the center, or open lines for rooks/bishops.
5. **Tactical Patterns**: Look for common tactical motifs like pins, forks, skewers, and discovered attacks.
6. **Fallback to Evaluation**: If no tactical opportunities are found, use a static evaluation function to score moves and pick the best one.

The evaluation function will be lightweight but effective, considering:
- Material balance (piece values)
- Piece activity (mobility, center control)
- King safety (pawn shield, open files)
- Pawn structure (isolated/doubled pawns, passed pawns)

The policy will use **memory** to cache evaluations or board states when possible to speed up repeated calculations.

### Key Features of the Policy:
1. **Checkmate Detection**: Uses a depth-1 search to find immediate checkmates.
2. **Tactical Prioritization**: Captures, checks, and promotions are scored highly.
3. **Material Evaluation**: Uses piece-square tables and material balance.
4. **King Safety**: Simplified pawn shield and center control evaluation.
5. **Efficiency**: Avoids full search trees by focusing on tactical opportunities first.
6. **Memory Usage**: Stores parsed legal moves for reuse (though in practice, legal moves would be provided per call).

### Limitations:
- The policy does not generate legal moves itself (assumes they are provided). In a real arena, you'd need to implement move generation or receive legal moves as input.
- The evaluation is simplified and may not handle complex positions well.
- No opening book or endgame tablebases are used.

This policy should perform well in tactical positions and midgames but may struggle in highly theoretical or endgame positions. Further optimizations could include deeper search for critical positions or more sophisticated evaluation heuristics.
