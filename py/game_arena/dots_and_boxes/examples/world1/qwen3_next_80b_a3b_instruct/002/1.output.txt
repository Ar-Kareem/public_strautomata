The logic for this policy is based on a hierarchical strategy that prioritizes safety, efficiency, and lookahead in the Dots and Boxes game on a 4x4 grid (5x5 dots):

1. **Winning Moves First**: Always check if any move completes a box (i.e., completes the 4th side of a box). If so, play that move to immediately gain points and retain the turn.

2. **Avoid Giving Away Free Boxes**: Never play a move that completes exactly 3 sides of any box unless it’s the only legal move. Such moves allow the opponent to capture a box on their turn — a dangerous gift.

3. **Safe Moves Priority**: Look for moves that are "safe" — meaning they don’t complete the third side of any box. Prioritize edges that are on the border or have few neighbors, as they are less likely to complete multiple boxes.

4. **Double-Cross Strategy (Advanced)**: If no safe moves exist and the opponent is forced to make a move, we may be forced to make a "sacrifice" — but only if it leads to a chain where we can capture multiple boxes afterward. We’ll use a minimal lookahead (1 move ahead) to detect if a move leads to a chain of captures.

5. **Edge Case Handling**: If all moves are dangerous (i.e., all complete a third side), then we choose the move that completes the fewest boxes (ideally just one), and prefer moves that minimize future opportunities for the opponent.

6. **Board State Awareness**: We consider the number of unclaimed boxes and remaining edges to determine if we’re in an endgame, but since the board is small (4x4 = 16 boxes, 40 total edges), a full search is feasible. However, for speed and simplicity, we rely on heuristic evaluation.

We implement a 3-tier selection:

- Tier 1: Any move that completes 1+ box → play it.
- Tier 2: Any move that doesn’t complete a 3rd side of any box → play the first such move.
- Tier 3: If no safe move exists, choose the move that completes the fewest boxes, prefer low-index edges for determinism.

We also avoid moves that would complete two boxes at once unless we are in a late-game endgame — but in 4x4, completing two boxes is actually beneficial unless it gives the opponent a chain. We’ll assume it’s beneficial if we score now, regardless.

This policy balances aggression with safety and is robust against naive opponents.
