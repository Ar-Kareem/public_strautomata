The logic for this policy is based on a hierarchical strategy that prioritizes safety, controls the game flow, and maximizes capture opportunities:

1. **Immediate Capture**: First, check if any move completes exactly one box (i.e., an edge that is the third side of a box). If such a move exists, take it—this gives an immediate point and an extra turn.

2. **Avoid Giving Away Boxes**: Never complete the third side of a box unless it’s forced (i.e., no other legal moves exist). A box with three sides filled is a gift to the opponent—giving them an extra turn and a point.

3. **Safe Edges**: Prefer edges that are on the border of the board or on boxes with zero or one side already drawn, minimizing the chance of creating a two-sided box that the opponent can exploit.

4. **Skeleton Strategy**: When no safe moves exist (all edges are part of at least two-sided boxes), then choose the move that minimizes the number of boxes it completes (i.e., if forced to complete a box, complete the fewest possible).

5. **Edge Priority**: Prioritize moves that are less likely to create double-cross opportunities. We scan the board systematically in row-major order to ensure determinism.

6. **Fallback**: If all else fails, we pick the first legal move that doesn't complete a box, or if none exists, the first legal move (even if it completes a box, because we’re forced).

This strategy mimics expert human play in Dots and Boxes — avoid giving your opponent double-cross opportunities, and only take boxes when you gain an extra turn. The policy is efficient, runs in O(1) per call (since the board is fixed 4x4 boxes = 5x5 edges), and is robust against common heuristics.
