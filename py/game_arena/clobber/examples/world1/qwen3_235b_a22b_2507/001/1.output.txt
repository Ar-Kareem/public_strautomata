The strategy is to implement a greedy heuristic that evaluates all legal moves and chooses the one that maximizes immediate capture opportunities while also considering mobility and board control. Since Clobber is a partisan, impartial-like game where every move captures an opponent's piece, the key is to avoid getting stuck with no moves.

We'll use the following approach:
1. Find all of our pieces.
2. For each of our pieces, check all four orthogonal directions.
3. If a direction leads to an opponent's piece (i.e., in bounds and opponent has a 1 there), it's a legal move.
4. Among all legal moves, we prioritize:
   - Moves that open up more future moves (i.e., increase mobility).
   - As a proxy, we prefer moves that land in central positions or that don't isolate our other pieces.
   - But for simplicity and robustness, we'll use a combination of:
     a) Prefer capturing an opponent piece that has fewer of our pieces adjacent to it (to avoid "wasting" a piece on an over-attacked target).
     b) Prefer moving pieces that currently have fewer escape options (i.e., low degree of mobility), to free them up.

However, due to the constraints (must always return a legal move, and only 1 second), we will implement a fast heuristic:

We'll score each move as:
score = - (number of our pieces adjacent to the target cell)
        + (bonus for moving a piece that currently has fewer possible captures)

Rationale:
- Capturing a target that is attacked by many of our pieces is "cheaper", so we do it later. So we prefer to capture targets that are attacked by few or no other pieces.
- We prefer to move a piece that currently has fewer options, to "free up" such pieces.

Fallback: if multiple moves have same score, pick the first (this ensures determinism).

We use only built-in Python and no external libraries (like numpy, since we don't need heavy computation and want speed).

We represent directions as offsets and map to string.

Important: Always double-check that the move is legal before returning.
