### Strategy for this Policy

**Lines of Action (LoA)** is a fascinating game that combines elements of **Gomoku** (connecting pieces) and **Poker** (moving pieces based on line lengths). The key to winning is balancing **contiguity** (connecting your own pieces) and **aggressive play** (forcing the opponent into a position where they cannot move optimally).

Here’s my strategy for this policy:

1. **Prioritize Contiguity**:
   - First, check if any of my pieces can be moved to connect two or more of my own pieces into a larger contiguous group. This is the core objective of the game.
   - If multiple such moves exist, prioritize the one that maximizes the size of the contiguous group I form.

2. **Aggressive Play (Forcing Opponent into Weak Moves)**:
   - If no immediate contiguity move is available, look for moves that would **increase the opponent’s line lengths** in a way that makes their future moves harder.
   - For example, if moving a piece would create a long line for the opponent (e.g., forcing them to move a piece that would otherwise stay still), do so to restrict their options.

3. **Defensive Play (Preventing Opponent from Winning)**:
   - If the opponent has a single contiguous group that is about to grow (e.g., they have a 2-piece line and can connect it), try to **block their contiguity** by moving my pieces in a way that disrupts their potential connections.
   - Avoid letting the opponent connect their pieces unless I can force a win in the next move.

4. **Center Control**:
   - Prefer moves near the center of the board (rows 3-4, columns 3-4) because they offer more opportunities for future contiguity and restrict the opponent’s mobility.

5. **Line Length Optimization**:
   - If no contiguity or blocking move is available, move pieces to **maximize my own line lengths** (similar to how in LoA, pieces must move as many squares as the length of the line they’re on).
   - This helps me create longer lines in future turns, allowing me to move more aggressively.

6. **Jump Over Friendly Pieces**:
   - If a move requires jumping over my own pieces (but not the opponent’s), prioritize it because it can lead to more flexible future moves.

7. **Capture Opponent’s Pieces**:
   - If a move allows me to capture the opponent’s piece (land on it), prioritize it to reduce their mobility and potentially force them into a weaker position.

8. **Fallback to Random Legal Move**:
   - If no strategic move is found, return a random legal move to avoid getting stuck.
