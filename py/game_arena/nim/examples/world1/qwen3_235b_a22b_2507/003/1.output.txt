The logic for this policy is based on the mathematical theory of the **misère variant of Nim**, where the player forced to take the last object **loses** (as opposed to normal play, where taking the last object wins). This is a *misère game*, and optimal strategy differs slightly from standard Nim.

In misère Nim with **all piles having size ≥ 2**, the optimal strategy is almost the same as normal Nim: aim to make the XOR (nim-sum) of all pile sizes zero. However, when **all piles are 1 or 0**, the parity (number of 1s) matters. Specifically:

- If exactly one or three piles are 1 (and the rest 0), the current player is in a losing position if perfect play is assumed.
- Near the end, when piles are reduced to many 1s, the strategy flips.

But since this is **misère**, the perfect strategy is:
1. If the game is in a state where **all piles are ≤ 1**, then you win if the number of 1s is even (since opponent will be forced to take the last one). So your goal is to leave an **odd** number of 1s.
2. Otherwise, play as in **normal Nim**: compute the nim-sum (XOR of all piles). If it's non-zero, make a move to set it to zero. **However**, in misère play, with all piles ≥ 2, you follow normal play until the endgame. The only adjustment is: **if a move would leave only piles of size 1 or 0**, avoid making the nim-sum zero if it would leave an even number of 1s. Instead, adjust to leave odd number of 1s.

But for **optimal play in misère Nim with four piles**, and given the initial state often has larger piles, the proven optimal strategy is:

> Play **normal Nim strategy (XOR = 0)** unless **all piles are ≤ 1**. And when you are forced to transition into the "all ≤1" phase, **adjust** so that the number of piles with 1 is **odd**.

    big_piles = [i for i in range(4) if piles[i] > 1]
    num_big = len(big_piles)
    
    # Case 1: no piles with more than 1 (all are 0 or 1)
