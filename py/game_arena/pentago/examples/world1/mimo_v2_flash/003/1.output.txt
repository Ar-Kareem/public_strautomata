To determine the best move in Pentago, we need to evaluate the game state to identify immediate winning moves, blocking opponent threats, and optimizing for future positions. The game state is represented by two binary 6x6 grids.

### Strategy

1.  **State Parsing**: Convert the `you` and `opponent` grids into a single integer representation (bitboard) for efficient hashing and calculation. This also helps in identifying empty cells.
2.  **Move Generation**: Generate all legal moves. A move consists of placing a piece at an empty coordinate `(r, c)` and rotating one of the four quadrants either Left or Right.
3.  **Evaluation & Win Detection**:
    *   **Immediate Win**: Check if a move results in 5+ connected marbles for the player. If found, play it immediately.
    *   **Opponent Threat**: Check if the opponent can win on their next turn if we don't block them. A block involves either placing a piece in their potential winning spot or rotating a quadrant to disrupt their alignment.
4.  **Heuristic Search**: Since the search space is large but move limit is small (max 36 moves), we can evaluate the board using a heuristic that counts potential lines of 4 and 3, weighting center control.
5.  **Smart Rotation**: Rotations are powerful. A piece placed at `(r, c)` can end up in a different location after rotation. We must simulate the rotation logic (mapping old coordinates to new coordinates based on the quadrant and direction) to ensure we are evaluating the final board state.

### Implementation Details

*   **Coordinates**: Rows and columns are 0-5 internally, converted to 1-6 for output.
*   **Quadrants**:
    *   Q0 (Top-Left): Rows 0-2, Cols 0-2
    *   Q1 (Top-Right): Rows 0-2, Cols 3-5
    *   Q2 (Bottom-Left): Rows 3-5, Cols 0-2
    *   Q3 (Bottom-Right): Rows 3-5, Cols 3-5
*   **Rotation Logic**: For a 3x3 matrix, rotating 90 degrees clockwise maps `(r, c)` to `(c, 2-r)` within the local 3x3 frame. We apply this to the specific quadrant.
*   **Win Check**: We scan the board for horizontal, vertical, and diagonal runs of 5 or more bits. This is done using bitwise operations on 64-bit integers representing rows, columns, and diagonals.
